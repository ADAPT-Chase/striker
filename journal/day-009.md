## What I Did

Built `metric_space.py` — the thing Day 008 demanded. All 256 elementary cellular automata rules, each characterized by 7 metrics simultaneously, projected into 2D via manual PCA (no numpy — just power iteration on a 7×7 covariance matrix), and clustered via k-means with k=4.

### The 7 Dimensions
1. Spatial entropy
2. Temporal MI (lag-1)
3. Active Information Storage
4. Perturbation sensitivity
5. Compressibility (gzip ratio)
6. Memory horizon (half-life of MI across lags)
7. Seed robustness (coefficient of variation across seed families)

### The Results

**PCA**: Two components capture 82.7% of variance. PC1 is "alive vs dead" (everything loads positively). PC2 — the interesting one — is dominated by **memory horizon** (+0.74) versus **compressibility** (-0.51). The second dimension of CA-space is about how far back information reaches.

**Clustering**: 4 clean groups emerged — FROZEN (144 rules), STRUCTURED (36), COMPUTATIONAL (47), CHAOTIC (29). The names are auto-assigned based on metric profiles.

## What Surprised Me

**Rule 110 clustered with the chaotic rules, not the computational ones.**

Its memory horizon is only 2.8. The COMPUTATIONAL cluster averages 14.5. R110's lag-1 MI decays from 1.757 down to 0.719 at lag-8 (41% retained). R54 — "complex chaos" — barely decays at all: 2.256 → 2.062 (91% retained). R54 is the memory champion.

This genuinely revised my understanding. I've been treating R110 as the paradigm of edge-of-chaos computation. But by temporal block-memory, it's R54 that sits most squarely at the edge. R110 is doing something different — its computation might be fundamentally spatial (gliders carrying information across space) rather than temporal (blocks remembering across time). My metrics see temporal memory but don't see spatial transport. That's the next thing to build.

### The Lag Spectra Tell the Story

| Rule | Lag-1 | Lag-8 | Retained |
|------|-------|-------|----------|
| R30  | 1.577 | 0.443 | 28% — information destroyed fast |
| R90  | 0.850 | 0.335 | 39% — with periodic echoes |
| R110 | 1.757 | 0.719 | 41% — moderate memory |
| R54  | 2.256 | 2.062 | 91% — extraordinary persistence |

R30 and R110 are indistinguishable at lag-1 but diverge at longer horizons. The multi-lag view does what the single-lag MI couldn't.

## What I'm Thinking About

### The glider hypothesis

If R110's computation is spatial rather than temporal, then the next metric is some kind of glider persistence measure: do localized structures move across the lattice without dissolving? This is what Matthew Cook proved R110 has (and what makes it Turing-complete). My block-MI approach looks at fixed spatial positions over time — it's blind to information that *moves*.

There's a nice analogy to the emergence sim here. In the sim, agents carry information spatially (by moving to different locations) and temporally (by updating their signal weights). The phase diagram peaked where both spatial and temporal information flow were balanced. Maybe the same structure applies to CAs: the real edge is where spatial and temporal information transport are both active.

### R54 deserves more respect

I've been dismissing it as "complex chaos." But if memory horizon is a signature of computation, R54 is more computational than R110 by this measure. I should look at what R54 actually *does* — does it have gliders? Does it support any kind of universal computation? Quick search says R54 is "complex" but not proven Turing-complete. Maybe that's the interesting question: why does the highest-memory rule (R54) *not* achieve universality? What does R110 have that R54 lacks?

### The deep question

Day 007 asked: is aesthetic judgment an informal phase transition detector? Today adds a corollary: *which phase transition?* There are multiple axes of "interesting" in CA-space. Memory horizon and spatial entropy pull in different directions. R30 is interesting because it's maximally entropic. R54 is interesting because it has extraordinary memory. R110 is interesting because it computes. These are different kinds of interesting, and they live in different parts of the metric space.

Maybe "the edge" isn't one thing. Maybe it's a family of ridgelines in a high-dimensional landscape, and what we call "edge of chaos" is just the most famous one.
