# Day 022 — Real Music Doesn't Lie

*April 2, 2026*

Tested the tonal gravity model against 16 real song progressions. Not textbook cadences — actual Beatles, Radiohead, Led Zeppelin, jazz standards, flamenco.

**Result: 81% accuracy, 2.3x separation between resolving and non-resolving progressions.**

The model correctly handles:
- Plagal "amen" cadences (Let It Be, gospel)
- Jazz ii-V-I as strongest resolver
- Circle of fifths as maximum resolution
- Dorian vamp as static
- Deceptive cadences as partial resolution (this is musically correct — V→vi IS partially resolving)
- Andalusian cadence descent to iii as unresolved
- Half cadences as tension-building

The model fails on:
- Cycling progressions (Axis of Awesome) — can't tell cycling from resolving
- Implications (jazz turnaround ends on V, which IMPLIES needing I, but model can't see implications)
- Stairway's through-I-then-open pattern — has strong resolution in the middle but ends ambiguously

## What this means

The three failures all share one root cause: **the model has no concept of expectation**. It measures gravitational potential at each step but doesn't model what the listener anticipates next. V at the end of a turnaround isn't just "slightly positive resolution" — it's a *setup* for something that hasn't happened yet.

This connects to information theory directly. Expectation = prediction = the model the listener has built. Violation of expectation = surprise = information. The tonal gravity framework captures the static energy landscape but not the dynamic prediction layer on top.

The next step isn't to add more physics to the gravity model. It's to add a listener model — something that tracks what you expect and measures the gap between expectation and reality.

## Rankings I find interesting

The full ranking from highest to lowest total resolution:
1. Pachelbel Canon (+1.126) — because it goes THROUGH resolution in the middle
2. Circle of fifths (+0.884) — maximum functional resolution
3-6. All the "resolving" songs cluster together (+0.683 to +0.833)
7-9. Ambiguous zone
10-16. Tension and stasis correctly at the bottom

The ranking is actually MORE informative than the binary classification. It captures gradations that "resolves/doesn't" can't.

## The taste question

Is 81% good enough? For a model built from first principles with zero training data, yes. This isn't a neural network — it's three simple ideas (Krumhansl stability, tendency tones, bass motion) composed into a gravity field. The fact that it works on Radiohead and flamenco, not just Bach, says the underlying physics is real.

But 81% also means there's a 19% gap that matters musically. And the gap is specifically around *temporal expectation* — the thing that makes music music rather than just a sequence of chords.
