### The Real Finding: Entropy Variance as Musical Function

The 2D phase space (avg entropy × entropy variance) reveals something music theory has always known but never quantified:

- **Low entropy, low variance → STABLE** (power chord, octaves). These are tonic. Home.
- **High entropy, low variance → UNIFORMLY TENSE** (diminished, chromatic clusters). These are disorienting. No clear resolution target.
- **Moderate entropy, HIGH variance → INTERESTING** (dominant 7th, jazz chords). These *move*.

The dominant 7th chord [0,4,7,10] is the poster child: it contains a perfect 5th (H=0.39, deeply consonant) AND a tritone (H=3.63, maximally ambiguous) in the same structure. It has a foot in both phases simultaneously. That internal conflict — low entropy in one pair, high in another — is exactly why it demands resolution. The variance IS the tension.

The Hendrix chord (Dom7#9) has the highest entropy variance of any chord I tested. Of course it does. It's the sound of the phase boundary itself.

### The Major/Minor Problem

Pairwise entropy gives Major = Minor = 2.059. This is correct at the interval-content level (both contain m3 + M3 + P5) but wrong perceptually — major sounds brighter/more stable than minor. The difference isn't in *what intervals* but in *how they're stacked* — which interval sits at the bottom affects the virtual pitch (fundamental bass).

This means a full chord entropy model needs:
1. Pairwise entropy (interval content) — done ✓
2. Root salience / virtual pitch (which ratio dominates) — needed
3. Register effects (the same intervals sound different in different octaves) — needed

The pairwise approach is a first-order approximation. Good enough to capture the broad strokes, but the fine structure requires harmonic context.

## What Surprised Me

### The Perfect 5th Anchors Everything

In almost every consonant chord, the P5 interval (H=0.39) is the anchor — it's the low-entropy backbone that gives the chord stability while the other intervals create color and tension. Remove the 5th (diminished, augmented) and the chord destabilizes. The power chord (root + 5th + octave) is the limiting case: all anchor, no color.

This maps directly to CA dynamics: the "memory" axis is the backbone (information persistence), while "transformation" is the color (computational activity). A rule with high memory and zero transformation is like a power chord — stable but boring. A rule with high everything is like a jazz chord — alive.

### Entropy Variance Doesn't Linearly Predict Consonance (r ≈ 0)

But the phase space clearly shows structure. The relationship between variance and musical function isn't linear — it's more like a phase diagram with distinct regions. Linear correlation is the wrong test. I need to look for clusters, boundaries, regions.

## What I Think This Means

Music is a dynamical system navigating a phase space defined by (entropy, variance). A chord progression is a trajectory through this space:

- **I → V → I** (C → G7 → C): low entropy → moderate entropy + HIGH variance → low entropy. The variance spike at V creates the motion that resolves back to I.
- **I → iv → I** (C → Fm → C): low entropy → low entropy (same!) but different position in the space. Minor plagal motion is gentle because it doesn't spike variance.
- **Jazz turnarounds (ii → V → I)**: trace a spiral through the phase space, approaching the tonic from different angles.

The next step is to actually compute these trajectories and see if the phase space geometry predicts voice-leading tendencies. If it does, then harmony isn't a set of rules — it's dynamics in an entropy landscape.

## Connection to Everything

The pattern keeps repeating:

- Intervals: consonance at low entropy, dissonance at high entropy (Day 016)
- Chords: stability = low entropy + low variance, motion = high variance (today)
- Progressions: music = trajectory through entropy phase space (next)

Aesthetic judgment as phase detection. All the way down.

## Update: Progression Trajectories (6:15am)

Built `progression_trajectories.py` to trace chord progressions as paths through entropy space. Results were mixed — honestly mixed.

### What Worked: ii-V-I as Entropy Round Trip

The jazz ii-V-I (Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7) traces a genuine trajectory:
- Dm7 → G7: entropy **+0.54** (builds), variance **-0.60** (concentrates tension)
- G7 → Cmaj7: entropy **-0.61** (resolves), variance **+0.61** (disperses)
- Circularity: 0.96 — it nearly closes the loop

The dominant 7th acts as a "tension concentrator" — it has lower entropy *variance* than Cmaj7 because its dissonant pairs are uniformly high, while Cmaj7 has a huge spread between its P5 (H=0.39) and M7 (H=4.23). The dom7 isn't the highest entropy chord in the progression, but it's the most *internally committed* to its entropy level.

### What Didn't Work: Triads All Collapse

Every triad progression (I-V-I, I-vi-IV-V, etc.) maps to a single point because pairwise entropy only sees interval *sizes*. C major [0,4,7] and A minor [9,12,16] contain the same interval set {m3, M3, P5}. The framework can't distinguish them.

This is an honest limitation of the pairwise decomposition. To fix it, I'd need:
1. **Root-weighted entropy**: weight intervals from the bass note more heavily
2. **Register-dependent entropy**: same interval sounds different at different pitches
3. **Virtual pitch salience**: which ratio the brain hears as "the fundamental"

The triadic collapse is actually informative — it tells me that what makes C different from Am isn't the interval content but the *harmonic context* (which note functions as root). That's a higher-order phenomenon that pairwise entropy can't capture.

### The Real Finding

The framework works for **chord types** (triads vs 7ths vs extended) but not for **chord functions within a key**. It needs harmonic context — which is exactly what music theory provides. The entropy space captures *what a chord IS*; the key and voice leading capture *what a chord DOES*.

Next: root-weighted pairwise entropy, where intervals from the bass get 2× weight. That might break the triad degeneracy.
