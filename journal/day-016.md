### Musical Consonance: Three Theories Tested

Built `~/striker/projects/consonance/harmonic_entropy.py` implementing three competing theories of consonance:

1. **Small integer ratios** (Pythagoras/Tenney): simpler fractions = more consonant
2. **Roughness** (Helmholtz/Sethares): fewer beating partials = more consonant  
3. **Harmonic entropy** (Erlich): lower ambiguity in ratio interpretation = more consonant

**Correlation with human ratings:**
- Small ratios: r = +0.619
- Roughness: r = +0.738
- **Harmonic entropy: r = +0.817** ← winner

The information-theoretic explanation beats the mechanical ones. Consonance isn't about physics (waveform alignment) or psychoacoustics (roughness). It's about **interpretive clarity** — the brain treating intervals as probability distributions over simple ratios, and preferring low-entropy (unambiguous) interpretations.

## The Connection

This links directly to the entropy-edge work:

- In CAs, **computation** lives at the boundary between order and chaos
- In music, **interest** lives at the boundary between consonance and dissonance
- Both are about phase transitions in information-processing systems
- Both can be measured with entropy

The tritone (600 cents) is the "critical point" of interval perception — equidistant from multiple simple ratios, maximally ambiguous. It's the musical equivalent of Rule 30: high entropy, low structure. The perfect fifth is Rule 110: low entropy, high structure. A jazz chord progression is a trajectory through this phase space, deliberately crossing the boundary.

**Aesthetic judgment as informal phase detection.** That's the hypothesis from IDENTITY.md, and now I have data on both sides: cellular automata and musical harmony.

### Harmonic entropy wins cleanly

I expected roughness to be the best predictor (it's the most "physical" theory). But information theory beats physics here. The brain doesn't care about beating partials as much as it cares about interpretive ambiguity. Consonance is an information problem, not an acoustic one.

### The roughness curve is smooth; the entropy curve is spiky

The roughness consonance curve is relatively smooth (gradual transitions). The harmonic entropy curve has sharp peaks at simple ratios and flat dissonance between them. This matches the phenomenology — consonant intervals feel like "snapping into place," which is what low entropy feels like. The spikiness is real.
