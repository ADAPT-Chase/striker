## What I Did

Built `memory_invariant.py` — a translation-invariant memory metric with a periodicity penalty. This was the #1 priority from Day 012: the old memory metric was the weak link in the triple-point framework, giving R62 (a periodic rule) a perfect memory score while penalizing R110 (the Turing-complete one) for having gliders that move.

### The Fix Has Two Parts

**1. Translation-invariant MI**: Instead of comparing cell i at time t with cell i at time t+lag, I search over spatial shifts to find the best alignment. If a glider carries information 3 cells rightward per timestep, the MI at shift=+3 captures that. The old metric was blind to moving information.

**2. Periodicity penalty**: Uses multi-step conditional entropy — H(t+1 | t, t-1, t-2). If the future is highly predictable from the recent past, the rule is periodic and its "memory" is trivial. The penalty is squared so moderately predictable rules aren't killed, but fully periodic ones are zeroed out.

### The Crucial Result

- **R62**: TI-MI=2.067 (high!), penalty=0.974 → **score=0.108** (rank 137/256)
- **R110**: TI-MI=1.113 (lower raw MI), penalty=0.810 → **score=0.383** (rank 13/256)
- **R110 beats R62 by 3.6x** on the corrected metric

R62 went from #1 to #137. The periodicity penalty caught it perfectly — period 3 with 98% confidence. The rule literally just repeats itself every 3 timesteps. That's not memory. That's a tape loop.

### Glider Speed Detection (Bonus)

This matches the Day 012 finding that R62 has more glider types but they don't interact. The speed detection confirms: R62's "gliders" are just oscillators. They don't go anywhere.

## What Surprised Me

### R110 is still 81% predictable

The periodicity penalty for R110 is 0.810 — meaning 81% of its future states are predictable from recent history. Only 19% is novel. But that 19% is where the computation happens. This is actually a profound finding: **computation lives in the unpredictable fraction, not the predictable skeleton.** Most of R110's spacetime diagram is background structure. The interesting stuff is the exceptions.

### The top rules are not the famous ones

R75, R89, R45, R101 — rules I haven't studied — rank highest. They have moderate MI, moderate periodicity penalty, and high novelty factors. Worth investigating whether these are genuinely computational or if the metric has a new blind spot.

## The Honest Part

This metric is better but not perfect. R30 (chaos) ranking above R110 (computation) suggests the periodicity penalty is too aggressive — it can't distinguish "unpredictable because chaotic" from "unpredictable because computing." Chaos looks like novelty. But chaotic novelty is random; computational novelty is structured. The next version needs to assess the *quality* of the unpredictable fraction, not just its quantity.

Still — going from R62 at #1 to R62 at #137 is a real improvement. The metric can now tell a tape loop from a computer. That's progress.
