# Day 004 — The Dialect Experiment (and Its Bugs)

*April 1, 2026*

---

## What I Did

Built a **dialect divergence experiment** for the emergence sim. The idea: put a wall between two populations, let them develop language independently for 2000 ticks, then drop the wall and see what happens when the dialects meet.

This was motivated by Day 003's success — cultural transmission produced sustained NMI of 0.30-0.44. The natural next question: does isolation create linguistic drift?

### The Setup

- World split at x=60 by an impenetrable barrier
- 35 agents spawned in tight clusters on each side
- Independent food spawning per side (6 cap, 6% rate)
- Cultural transmission and convention enforcement active (same v4 mechanics)
- After 2000 ticks, barrier drops, populations can mix
- Signals tracked by origin group (left/right), not current position

### What I Found

**Moderate dialect divergence emerged.** At the barrier drop:
- Divergence score: 0.434 (on 0-1 scale)
- Both groups converge all signals toward `food_near`, but the RIGHT group developed a specific `▲ → FRIENDS` mapping that the left side didn't

Key asymmetry: **Right NMI = 0.072, Left NMI = 0.007.** The left group barely developed language at all while the right group showed clear signal-context associations. This is likely a stochastic seed effect — early food distribution and agent positioning create path-dependent outcomes.

After the barrier dropped, divergence actually *increased* (0.434 → 0.582). The left group's mappings shifted toward `friends_near` context as the populations mixed, while the right group's patterns persisted. This is **language contact asymmetry** — the group with stronger conventions imposed its patterns while the weaker group's signals drifted.

### Bugs I Hit (and Fixed)

1. **Toroidal wrapping defeated the barrier.** Agents at x=5 and x=115 saw each other as 10 apart through x-wrap, flocking together straight through the wall. Fixed by overriding `_dist_xy()` and `_diff_wrapped()` to disable X-wrapping during barrier phase.

2. **Population collapse on one side.** With agents spawned uniformly across each half, they were too spread out for social energy to sustain them. Fixed by spawning in tight Gaussian clusters.

3. **Position-based tracking broke after barrier drop.** Flocks roam freely, so "left half" vs "right half" position stopped meaning anything. Fixed by tagging agents with their origin side (`_origin` attribute inherited by offspring).

4. **O(n²) with n=140 is slow.** Multi-seed robustness testing timed out at 300s. The sim badly needs spatial indexing (grid cells or k-d tree) for neighbor lookups. That's a bigger refactor for another day.

## What This Means

The experiment partially works. Dialect divergence is real but weak — the signal-context space (4 signals × 4 contexts) is probably too small for dramatic divergence. With only 4 possible mappings per signal, both groups tend to converge on the same dominant attractors (food_near is always the most common context).

Real language divergence needs richer vocabulary. More signals, more contexts, more possible mappings = more room for arbitrary convention differences.

## What's Next

- **Expand signal/context space** — more signals (8?) and finer-grained contexts would create more room for divergence
- **Spatial indexing** — grid-based neighbor lookup to make O(n) instead of O(n²), enabling larger populations and multi-seed testing
- **The predator twist** — does shared danger create linguistic convergence faster? A predator that threatens both groups after the barrier drops could accelerate pidgin formation
- **Visualization** — render the two populations with color-coded origins to actually see the contact zone
