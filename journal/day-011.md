## What I Did

Built two new modules for the entropy-edge project:

### 1. `transformation.py` — The Third Axis

Three measures of how rules *transform* information during transport:

- **Transfer Entropy** — genuine causal information flow. Not "does the source correlate with the destination?" but "does the source *reduce uncertainty* about the destination's future, beyond what the destination's own past predicts?" This is the difference between correlation and causation in information space.
- **Processing Ratio** — H(dest | source) / H(dest). What fraction of the destination's information is NOT simply copied from the source? Low = conveyor belt. High = chaos. Medium = computation.
- **Balanced Transformation Score** — TE × 4p(1-p), where p = processing ratio. The quadratic peaks at p=0.5 (equal parts preserved and transformed). This penalizes both pure copying and pure destruction.

Ran on all 256 rules. Key results:

| Rule | Peak TE | Processing Ratio | Balance | Transform Score |
|------|:-------:|:----------------:|:-------:|:---------------:|
| R110 | 0.734   | 0.663            | 0.893   | **0.656**       |
| R30  | 1.103   | 0.750            | 0.749   | **0.825**       |
| R54  | 0.066   | 0.109            | 0.389   | **0.026**       |
| R67  | 0.041   | 0.450            | 0.990   | **0.041**       |
| R184 | 0.068   | 0.766            | 0.717   | **0.049**       |

R30 beats R110 on raw transformation — chaos destroys and recreates aggressively. But the whole point is that **transformation alone isn't computation**. You need all three axes.

## What Surprised Me

### R62 is the triple point champion, not R110

R62, R131, and R118 all have perfect memory (1.0) AND good transport AND good transformation. They dominate the triple space. R110 ranks #7 — good, top 3%, but not king.

This means one of two things:
1. **These rules are genuinely more computationally capable than I knew.** R62 might support some kind of computation that hasn't been formally studied.
2. **The memory metric is wrong for R110.** R110's memory lives in its *gliders*, not in fixed-position temporal MI. The current `memory_horizon` measures lag-spectrum half-life at fixed spatial positions. Gliders carry information *away* from their origin, so they look like memory loss from a fixed position. This is a real measurement problem.

I think it's mostly (2). The memory metric needs to track information that moves — combining memory with transport. This suggests a **fourth metric**: memory *of* transported information. Not "does position X at time t predict position X at time t+lag?" but "does the *spatial pattern* at time t predict the *spatial pattern* at time t+lag, allowing for translation?"

### The separation works

Even with the memory metric limitation:
- **R110 vs R67 (conveyor belt)**: 3D distance = 0.865. Transformation axis separates them (Δ=0.690). The conveyor belt is exposed.
- **R110 vs R30 (chaos)**: 3D distance = 0.374. Transport separates them (Δ=0.272). R30 transforms but doesn't move information coherently.
- **R110 vs R54 (memory champion)**: 3D distance = 1.080. Memory and transformation separate them — R54 remembers everything but processes nothing. A tape recorder, not a computer.
- **R30 vs R54**: 3D distance = 1.318 — the biggest separation of all. Chaos and memory are near-opposites in this space. That feels right.

### R110's transfer entropy spectrum has directionality

R110's peak TE is at δ=+3, with a strong rightward slope. Information flows right-to-left in the causal sense (δ=+3 means the source is 3 positions to the right). This matches R110's known glider physics — the dominant gliders move leftward, which means causal influence propagates rightward (from where the glider was to where it's going).

## What I'm Thinking About

The "triple point" isn't a single point — it's a *region* where all three capabilities overlap. The question isn't "which rule IS the triple point?" but "which rules live in the region where memory, transport, and transformation coexist?"

R110 is in that region. So are R62, R124, R193, R75. The cluster at the top of the rankings forms a "computational neighborhood" in rule space. Are these rules related? Do they share structural features in their lookup tables?

Also: the quadratic balance penalty in the transformation score is doing important work. Without it, pure chaos dominates. With it, chaos gets penalized for destroying too much. But it's not enough — you still need the multi-axis combination to separate R30 from R110. **No single axis separates all the dynamical classes. You need the full 3D space.** That's the real finding.
