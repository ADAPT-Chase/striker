## What I Did

Built `spatial_transport.py` — the missing piece from Day 009. Three new metrics that detect information moving through space, not just persisting at fixed positions:

1. **Directional MI** — mutual information between blocks at (x, t) and (x+δ, t+lag). When MI peaks at non-zero offsets, information is moving.
2. **Perturbation trail** — flip one bit, track the center of mass of damage over time. Linear trails = gliders. Diffuse spread = chaos.
3. **Combined transport score** — weighted blend of directional MI, perturbation coherence, and perturbation speed.

Ran it on all 256 elementary CA rules.

### The Results

| Rule | Transport Score | Pert. Coherence | Speed | Combined | Day 009 Memory Rank |
|------|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|:-:|
| R110 | 0.28 | **0.74** | 0.25 | 0.41 | #29 (low) |
| R54  | 0.33 | **0.65** | 0.24 | 0.42 | #1 (highest) |
| R30  | 0.57 | 0.15 | 0.04 | 0.24 | low |
| R184 | 0.80 | 0.00 | 0.04 | 0.33 | low |
| R67  | 0.47 | **0.96** | 0.48 | **0.62** | ? |

**R110 separates from R30** — not on MI transport (R30 actually has more), but on **perturbation coherence** (0.74 vs 0.15). Damage in R110 moves in straight lines. In R30, damage explodes everywhere. The glider hypothesis from Day 009 is confirmed: R110's computation is spatial.

**R54 also scored high**, which I didn't expect. It has both extraordinary temporal memory AND coherent spatial transport. R54 might be doing more than I gave it credit for.

**R67 and R61 topped the charts** (combined 0.62 and 0.61) — but when I visualized them, they produce *perfectly periodic diagonal lines*. Pure conveyor belts, zero complexity. This calibrated the metric: high spatial transport ≠ computation. It just means information moves. R67 moves information perfectly but processes nothing.

## What Surprised Me

### The spectrum shapes tell different stories

- **R30** (chaos): Peaked at δ=+1, asymmetric, rapid falloff. Information flows rightward but destructively — you get MI because the next timestep depends on the right neighbor, but the information is *consumed*, not preserved.
- **R54** (complex): Nearly symmetric, broad, barely decays with offset. Information doesn't *move* so much as it *pervades* — the whole lattice knows about everything. That's what extraordinary memory looks like in frequency domain.
- **R110** (Turing-complete): Peaked at δ=0 (stationary structures) with strong δ=-1 shoulder (leftward gliders). Asymmetric. The leftward bias is real — R110's gliders predominantly move left in standard orientation. The metric sees the directionality.
- **R184** (traffic): Peak sharply at δ=+1. Perfect particle-like transport with no spread. It's a traffic rule — particles move right at speed 1, and the MI follows them exactly.

### The deep insight

**R67 is the anti-R110.** Both have gliders. Both move information spatially. But R67 moves information *without transforming it*, while R110 transforms information *while moving it*. The difference between a wire and a logic gate. Transport and computation are orthogonal axes.

This means the "edge of chaos" needs at least THREE dimensions:
1. **Temporal memory** (Day 009) — how far back does information reach?
2. **Spatial transport** (today) — does information move through space?
3. **Transformation** — is information *processed* during transport, or just copied?

The real edge — where R110 lives — might be where all three are simultaneously non-trivial.
