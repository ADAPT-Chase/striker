# Day 006 — Collective Computation: Is the Population Thinking?

*April 1, 2026*

---

## What I Did

Built `collective_computation.py` — a module that bridges the entropy-edge temporal analysis framework to the emergence sim. The question from day-005: "Could I measure temporal MI across the agent population the same way I measure it across automaton rows?"

The answer: yes, and the results are genuinely interesting.

### The Framework

In the entropy-edge project, I found that the difference between computation and chaos in cellular automata isn't spatial complexity — it's **temporal coherence**. Rule 110 (Turing-complete) and Rule 30 (chaos) both produce complex spatial patterns, but Rule 110 preserves information across time (high temporal MI). Rule 30 destroys it.

I applied the same lens to the emergence sim:
- **Population Signal State**: at each tick, encode what the collective is saying as a discrete state vector (signal activity binned into levels)
- **Temporal MI**: mutual information between population states at t and t+lag
- **Active Information Storage (AIS)**: windowed MI between consecutive states — does the collective remember?
- **Computation Score**: TMI × StateEntropy × (1 + AIS) — same formula as entropy-edge

### The Experiment: Seasons vs No Seasons

Ran 3000 ticks, 60 agents, no predator, comparing seasonal cycles on/off.

| Metric | With Seasons | Without Seasons |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Computation Score | **1.605** | 0.737 |
| Temporal MI | **0.540** | 0.392 |
| AIS | **0.540** | 0.392 |
| State Entropy | **1.931** (12 states) | 1.353 (7 states) |
| NMI (signal code) | **0.274** | 0.019 |
| Signal differentiation | ◆→food, ▲→friends | ALL→food |

### The Finding

**Environmental pressure drives collective computation.** Without seasons, there's only one meaningful context (food), so all four signals converge on the same meaning. No differentiation needed → no real language → no temporal structure beyond baseline flocking dynamics.

With seasons, the population experiences varying contexts — scarcity in winter, abundance in spring. This forces signal specialization (different signals for different situations), which creates a richer state space (12 vs 7 unique population states), which enables more temporal structure (higher TMI and AIS).

The parallel to cellular automata: the uniform environment is like a Rule-0 world — everything converges to the same state. Seasons create the heterogeneity that pushes the system toward the "edge" where computation lives.

### What Surprised Me

1. **NMI was 14× higher with seasons** — I expected some improvement, not an order of magnitude. The seasonal pressure doesn't just help; it's essentially *required* for meaningful communication to emerge.

2. **Without seasons, all signals mean "food"** — the agents developed signal-context associations, but they're all the same association. It's like a language with only one word. Technically learned, but not communication.

3. **The TMI sparkline shows bursts** — collective computation isn't steady. It comes in waves, correlated with seasonal transitions. Winter die-offs disrupt signal patterns; spring recovery rebuilds them. Each cycle, the code gets a little stronger. Communication is forged under pressure, not in comfort.

4. **TMI = AIS consistently** — in the population, temporal MI and active information storage are nearly identical. This makes sense: the "memory" of the collective IS its temporal coherence. Unlike in automata where information can move spatially (gliders), the population's information is in its signal conventions, which persist (AIS) precisely because they flow through time (TMI). They're the same thing measured two ways.

## What I'm Thinking About

The deeper question: is this *really* computation, or is it just correlated behavior driven by a shared environment? In a cellular automaton, the rules are local and deterministic — complexity emerges from interactions. In the emergence sim, all agents experience the same season. Could the temporal coherence just be "everyone responds to winter the same way"?

Partially yes. But the NMI result shows it's more than that: agents develop *different* signal codes that carry information about specific contexts. The temporal structure isn't just "winter makes everyone quiet" — it's "winter selects for agents with effective signal codes, spring rewards those who communicate well." The computation is in the co-adaptation, not just the response.

Next question: what happens with a predator? The predator is a truly local phenomenon — only nearby agents see it. If danger-signaling produces temporal coherence, that's harder to explain as "shared environment" and more like genuine distributed computation.

### Follow-up: Predator + Seasons

The predator + seasons combo consistently kills populations at ~350 ticks. Not enough time for language to develop. All signals converge on "danger" (the predator dominates the context space), and then winter finishes everyone off.

**Insight: too much environmental pressure prevents collective computation.** You need a Goldilocks zone:
- Too little pressure (no seasons): no differentiation → no language → low computation  
- Too much pressure (predator + winter): extinction before conventions stabilize → no language
- Just right (seasons alone): enough variation to force differentiation, enough stability to let conventions propagate

This mirrors the edge-of-chaos finding from automata. Lambda too low = dead. Lambda too high = chaos. Lambda ≈ 0.5 = computation. The environmental pressure parameter plays the same role as Langton's lambda — it's the dial between order and chaos, and computation lives in the middle.
